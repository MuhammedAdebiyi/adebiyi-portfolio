the story so far

How CourseVault got built.

It started from a simple, annoying problem: students drowning in PDFs, slides and lecture notes, with no fast way to turn that mess into something they could actually study from. CourseVault was my answer an AI study platform that takes whatever you upload and turns it into structured, usable knowledge.

Under the hood it's a Next.js frontend, a .NET API, and a Python worker fleet talking over RabbitMQ. Every upload moves through a five-stage pipeline hash check, extraction, chunking, AI routing, knowledge asset creation and gets routed to whichever model handles that subject best: Gemini for humanities and medicine, DeepSeek for STEM, with Groq, OpenAI and Grok in the rotation too. Postgres holds the data, Redis handles caching and real-time state, R2 stores the files, and SignalR pushes live progress and powers collaborative study rooms.

It hasn't been quiet. I've fought a production server that fell over from OOM on a 1GB box, a Redis connection silently broken by hairpin NAT, race conditions between RabbitMQ and Postgres, and Vercel quietly killing long-lived connections which is why file processing now polls instead of streaming. Most of those fires got put out at night, alone, with a terminal and too much coffee.

CourseVault now runs on its own Hetzner box, and I'm still the only one shipping it frontend, backend, infra, and the AI pipeline in between. It's slower than having a team, but every part of the system is something I understand completely, which has turned out to be its own kind of fast.
